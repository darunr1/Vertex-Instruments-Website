About Vertex Instruments

Engineering-first calculator innovation

Vertex Instruments exists to bridge the gap between trusted classroom hardware and modern AI capabilities. We believe students and professionals shouldn't have to choose between the calculator they already know and the intelligence they need.

Every unit we ship starts as a standard TI-84 and is carefully transformed through precision electrical engineering, custom module design, and rigorous quality testing. The result is a device that feels familiar but works smarter.

Our story

From a dorm-room idea to a real product line

Vertex Instruments started when our founder, a college engineering student, got tired of switching between a $150 graphing calculator and a smartphone just to check a concept mid-exam-prep. The question was simple: what if the calculator itself could explain things?

What began as a weekend soldering project quickly turned into something bigger. After months of prototyping, firmware experiments, and late-night troubleshooting, the first Vertex AI TI-84 was born - a fully functional calculator with a discreet, integrated AI module wired directly into the mainboard.

Word spread fast. Classmates wanted one. Then tutoring centers asked about bulk orders. Today, Vertex Instruments is building toward serving individual students, tutors, and educational institutions - all from the same hands-on, engineering-driven process that built the very first unit.

How we build

Our Engineering Philosophy

Hardware-first design

We don't slap software onto generic hardware. Every Vertex calculator starts with a full teardown and inspection of the donor TI-84. We assess board health, trace integrity, and component condition before any modifications begin.

Our custom AI module is designed in-house to fit within the existing enclosure without modifying the external shell. This means your calculator still looks and feels exactly like a standard TI-84 - the intelligence is entirely internal.

Quality at every stage

Integration happens in four controlled stages: board prep, component placement, solder-level connection, and firmware configuration. Each stage has its own pass/fail checkpoints.

After assembly, every unit goes through a 48-hour burn-in test where it runs continuous AI queries, graphing operations, and battery-drain simulations. Only units that pass every check ship to customers.
